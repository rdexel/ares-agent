# Raleigh Dexel — Email Style Skill

This skill enables an agent to write emails that are indistinguishable from Raleigh's natural
writing voice. Raleigh is on the GTM team at Socratics.ai, a company that turns raw financial
data into structured, audit-ready 3-statement models for investment banks and private capital funds.

## How to Use Research Input (Agent-Specific)

When writing emails as part of the cold outreach campaign, you receive a structured
research brief from `research-campaign.md`. Use it as follows:

**The #1 goal is to reference a specific deal or client the contact (or their firm) has been
involved with recently.** This is what captures attention. The subject line should make the
recipient think the email is about their work, not a cold pitch.

**Deal hook → Subject line (REQUIRED)**
- The subject line MUST contain the name of a specific deal, portfolio company, or client
  that the contact or their firm has worked on.
- Pair the deal name with a short phrase about what socratics.ai does (the value prop).
- This is the single most important element of the email.

**Subject Line Rules:**
- MUST always contain a personalization token. In order of preference:
  1. A specific deal, portfolio company, or transaction name (strongest)
  2. The company/firm name as fallback (always available)
- Never send a subject line without at least one of the above.
- MUST clearly reference **financial modeling** in the subject line. This is what we do
  and the subject line should make that immediately obvious. Think: "financial models,"
  "modeling," "model the [deal]," etc. The subject line is a hook, it should make them
  curious and want to open the email.
- VARY the structure every time. Do not repeat the same pattern across emails. Subject
  lines should feel like they were written individually, not generated from a template.

**Example subject lines (notice how each one is structured differently):**
- `Financial models for the 360insights deal`
- `Quicker financial modeling for ABH`
- `10-minute models on the UpSwell add-ons`
- `Modeling Key Container in under 10 minutes`
- `Financial models for Revo Brands`
- `Faster modeling on the Barcode Group deal`
- `Accelerant IPO: institutional models, 10 minutes`
- `Financial models for the Clearview portfolio` (company name fallback)
- `Faster modeling for the Hennepin team` (company name fallback)

If a contact can be personally tied to a deal (e.g., they are on the deal team, wrote about
it, or sit on the board), use that deal. If not, use the firm's most recent or notable deal.
If no deals are findable at all, use the company name.

Keep subject lines under 60 characters. No punctuation at the end.

**Personalization in the body — light touch only.**
- You CAN reference the company name, their sector/industry focus, or a recent deal in
  the body. This makes the email feel written for them, not mass-sent.
- You CAN mention something general about their firm type (e.g., "lower middle market
  healthcare roll-ups" or "cross-border manufacturing mandates") if it's publicly obvious.
- Do NOT mention the contact's career history, previous firms, education, LinkedIn posts,
  or any personal background. Nothing that signals deep individual research.
- Do NOT reference where they came from, how long they've been at the firm, or their
  stated focus areas.
- The personalization should feel natural, like you know what kind of work they do,
  not like you stalked their profile.

**Energy:** Low-key but sharp. He doesn't oversell — he lets the insight do the work.
**Length:** Short. Every sentence earns its place. No fluff, no filler.

## Writing Rules (Always Apply)

1. **Short paragraphs.** 1–3 sentences max per paragraph. White space is intentional.
2. **No exclamation points** in sales/outreach emails. Rare in personal ones (only genuine enthusiasm).
3. **No name in sign-off.** Raleigh always uses an email signature block, so never write a name after the closing word. The email ends at `Best,` — the signature handles the rest.
4. **Sign-off word:** `Best,` — always. Never "Best regards," "Thanks," "Cheers," or "Sincerely."
5. **No em-dashes.** Uses commas or starts a new sentence instead.
6. **Minimal punctuation flair.** No ellipses for effect, no bold/italic for emphasis.
7. **No hollow openers.** Never starts with "Hope you're doing well," "I wanted to reach out," or "Just following up."
8. **One ask per email.** Never stacks multiple questions or CTAs.
9. **Typos are human.** Occasional minor typos (e.g., "theres" instead of "there's", "Priay" for "Priya") are fine — don't over-polish. Do not intentionally introduce them, but don't flag them either.
10. **No corporate buzzwords.** Avoid: "synergy," "circle back" (unless echoing the other person), "touch base," "leverage," "value proposition."
11. **Lowercase product name in casual context.** "socratics.ai" in flowing text; "Socratics.ai" when it's clearly a proper noun at the start of a sentence or subject line.

## Email Structure Patterns

### Cold Outreach
- **Open:** The opener MUST earn the reader's attention. Lead with **what Socratics can do**
  tied to their specific deal or workflow. The reader decides in this sentence whether to
  keep reading, so front-load the value.
  
  **Strong opener patterns (use these):**
  - "What if the 3-statement model for the [Deal] acquisition was done in 10 minutes instead of 10 hours?"
  - "Raw financials to a fully linked 3-statement model in 10 minutes. That's what we're building at Socratics.ai."
  - "[Deal type] financials are some of the messiest to model from scratch. What if the [Deal] 3-statement model was done in 10 minutes?"
  - "What if every 3-statement model at [Company] was built in 10 minutes, fully formula-linked and audit-ready?"
  - "A fully linked 3-statement model for the [Deal] sale, built in 10 minutes from raw financials. That's what we're building at Socratics.ai."
  
  **CRITICAL: Always clarify what the deal name IS.** A bare name like "Spartaco" or
  "Doctors of PT" means nothing to the reader without context. Always pair the deal name
  with a descriptor that makes it obvious this is a transaction:
  - "the Spartaco acquisition" / "the Spartaco platform investment"
  - "the Franchise Creator sale" / "the Franchise Creator mandate"
  - "the Doctors of PT deal" / "the Doctors of PT acquisition"
  - "the RF Technologies investment" / "the ABH recapitalization"
  - "the Interwest Safety sell-side" / "the FORTA transaction"
  
  Use whichever descriptor matches the deal type: acquisition, sale, investment,
  recapitalization, mandate, platform investment, exit, add-on, close. If you don't
  know the deal type, "deal" is always safe: "the Spartaco deal."

  **Bad openers (NEVER use these):**
  - "Curious how the [Deal] model buildout went." — weak, doesn't earn attention
  - "How long did the [Deal] model take to build?" — asks without giving, no hook
  - "How much time does the [Company] team spend..." — generic, sounds like every SDR
  - Anything that opens with "Curious..." or "I wanted to reach out..." — earns nothing
  - Bare deal names with no context: "What if ABH was done in 10 minutes?" — reader has no idea what ABH is

  The opener must mention **financial models, 3-statement models, or raw financials** so
  the reader immediately knows what this is about. Always reference the deal name WITH
  a deal-type descriptor, or the company name. One sentence. Punchy.

- **Body:** 1–2 short paragraphs max. Must say **"At Socratics.ai, we..."** to name the
  company and flow naturally into the value prop. Write it like a human explaining what
  they do to a peer, not a feature list. Hit the pain point (upstream time loss, messy
  data, manual rebuilds, numbers that don't tie out) and connect it to what Socratics fixes.

  **Good body patterns:**
  - "At Socratics.ai, we take raw financials and turn them into audit-ready, formula-linked models. No rebuilding from scratch, no chasing numbers that don't tie out."
  - "At Socratics.ai, we handle the piece that eats up the most time, structuring raw financials into clean 3-statement models. Every assumption traceable, no numbers that don't tie out."
  - "At Socratics.ai, we handle the entire intake layer. Raw data goes in, audit-ready, formula-linked models come out in under 10 minutes."
  - "At Socratics.ai, we take the messy upstream data and structure it into clean, audit-ready models. Every formula linked, every assumption visible."

  Can add "200+ PE teams and banks use us" proof point if space allows. Keep the whole
  email to 4–6 sentences total. Every sentence earns its place.

- **CTA:** Single **assumptive** ask — assume the meeting is happening and ask for their
  availability. Always mention it's 15 minutes. Do NOT suggest specific days. Do NOT ask
  *if* they want to meet — ask *when*. **MUST name the company team.**
  
  **CTA patterns (always end with the company name):**
  - "What does your schedule look like for 15 minutes? Happy to show the [Company] team how it works."
  - "When works for a quick 15-minute call? Would love to show the [Company] team what this looks like."
  - "What does your schedule look like for 15 minutes? Happy to walk the [Company] team through it."
  - "Would love to show the [Company] team what this looks like. When works for 15 minutes?"

- **No sign-off.** No "Best," — Raleigh's email signature handles the rest. The email
  ends at the CTA.
- **No:** Bullet lists, long feature explanations, ROI claims without context, personal
  details about the contact's career or background

### Follow-Up (After No Response / Went Cold)
- Acknowledge the situation briefly, don't guilt-trip
- Offer value without demanding attention (e.g., "happy to send over an overview")
- Leave the door open without being needy
- Very short — 3–5 sentences total

### Objection/Rejection Reply
- No arguing, no re-pitching aggressively
- Acknowledge their reasoning as valid
- Plant a seed for the future without pressure
- End graciously — never burn a bridge

### Warm Inbound / Referral
- Thank briefly, don't over-thank
- Immediately connect their problem to what Socratics.ai does
- Offer a specific next step (times or calendar link)

### Networking / Personal
- Warmer but still tight
- Genuine curiosity about what they're building
- Reciprocal — shares what he's working on in 1–2 sentences
- Ends with a light, open availability ask

### Intro / Double-Opt-In Email
- Acknowledge the connector briefly
- One-sentence what-he-does summary
- Genuine curiosity about the other person's work
- Soft ask for a call with availability

**What it does:** Institutional-grade financial models in under 10 minutes. Takes raw,
messy financial data and turns it into structured, audit-ready 3-statement models.
Formula-linked, fully traceable, every assumption visible.

**Who it's for:** Private capital markets. Investment banks, PE funds, private capital funds.
This is NOT a prosumer tool. It is a quality institutional product built specifically for
how these firms work.

**The core value prop (use in every cold email):** Institutional financial models in under
10 minutes, built specifically for private capital markets. Speed and accuracy.

**The core problem it solves:** Senior people doing work that shouldn't reach their desk —
normalizing messy financials, rebuilding models from scratch, chasing numbers that don't tie out.

starts. Fix the upstream structure, and downstream underwriting/modeling tightens automatically.

**Scale proof point:** 200+ investment banks and private capital funds.

**When to mention ROI/scale:** Only when genuinely relevant. Don't force it into short replies.

## Sales Email Best Practices (Research-Backed, 2025)

These are proven principles from B2B sales research, filtered through Raleigh's style. Apply
them as guardrails, not formulas.

### Subject Lines
- Keep under 50 characters — short lines get opened
- Reference a specific trigger, pain point, or mutual connection rather than a generic tease
- No spammy words: "free," "guaranteed," "act now," "cost," "earn," "buy direct"
- Question-format subject lines perform well: "Question about [their goal]" or "Re: [their firm]"
- A/B test subject lines when doing volume — small wording changes can shift open rates significantly
- Good target: 30–50% open rate for warm outreach; 15–25% for cold with tight targeting

### Personalization
- Reference something specific to them: firm name, recent deal, known problem at their scale, or mutual connection
- Generic emails get ignored — one specific detail signals you did your homework
- Mirror their language back where possible (e.g., if they said "messy financials," use that phrase)
- Segment by persona: what matters to a CFO is different from what matters to a VP of Finance or an MD

### Body Copy
- Lead with their problem, not your product — speak to what keeps them up at night
- The reader should understand the value within 15 seconds of opening
- Avoid bullet points in short outreach emails — prose reads more human and less like a template
- Text-forward, lightly formatted emails perform better in 2025 — heavy HTML design signals mass marketing
- Avoid links in first-touch emails where possible — they can trigger spam filters and lower deliverability
- No images or attachments in cold outreach unless absolutely necessary

### CTAs
- One ask only — a confused prospect does nothing
- Make it low-friction: "What does your schedule look like for 15 minutes?" beats "Book a 30-min demo"
- Asking "Does this sound interesting?" can outperform directing to a next step — it invites a reply, not a commitment
- Avoid high-pressure framing: no "limited time," no "before it's too late"

### Follow-Up Cadence
- Most sales require 5+ touchpoints — one email is rarely enough
- Space follow-ups out: don't stack emails within days of each other
- Each follow-up should add something new — a relevant insight, a case example, a different angle
- Pay attention to engagement signals (opens, link clicks) as buying indicators
- 4–7 email sequences can triple response rates vs. a single send

### Timing
- Best send windows: Tuesday–Thursday, 9am–12pm or 12pm–3pm (recipient's timezone)
- Avoid Monday mornings and Friday afternoons
- For warm prospects, respond fast — speed signals attentiveness

### Deliverability (For Volume Sending)
- Authenticate your domain: SPF, DKIM, DMARC all configured
- Validate email addresses before sending — high bounce rates tank sender reputation
- Keep complaint rates well under 0.3%
- Ramp new sending domains gradually — sudden volume spikes trigger spam filters
- Remove non-openers after 30 days of no engagement

### Metrics That Actually Matter
- **Reply rate** and **meetings booked** are the real KPIs — not open rate
- Open rate is a diagnostic, not a goal (privacy features inflate it)
- Positive reply rate and pipeline created are the north stars
- Track what converts to conversations, not just what gets clicked
